  • 2021-04-06 Fishing tip #9: setting the hook. Setting the hook means to set the hook into the fishes mouth, it digs the hook in deep enough that the barb will pass through the mouth. On small bites where the pole just barely twitches for a split second, it would be advised to avoid setting the hook, however, the hook still can be set in this situation. It is best to avoid setting small bites when the lure is something that would attract bites away from the hook, as the fish are likely not biting hard enough for the hook to enter their mouths. Lures like this are jigs, or lures with the hook in the center of the lure. Small bites should be hooked at times when a lure with a hook at the back end of the lure is used. Examples of this are spinners, spoons, wedding ring style lures, or anything else with a hook towards the back. These types of lures should be hooked with small bites because even if the fish is biting a small portion of the lure, it is likely still biting the hook. Any bite that physically moves the tip of the pole a visible amount should at least be attempted to be hooked. When setting the hook, jerk the pole back, unless weight suddenly drops in the middle of the jerk in which you would drop the line back down and try for another bite. If, after setting the hook, there is still a fish on, reel in.

  • 2021-04-26 Fishing tip #14: typical weight for fishing? For average conditions and average gear being used, you will usually use less than a quarter ounce of weight. But, it should be remembered that when it comes to weight, less is best. Can it sometimes depend on reaction time or personal preference? My use of weights is almost always personal preference, however, I rely on certain conditions such as wind and desired depth to determine if I need more weight. What is the weight that is always okay to use? For use on shore, small split shot sinkers about the size of a pea work perfectly. This weight can be decreased or increased based on personal preference, wind, weight of used lure, and method of fishing, (casting, jigging, etc.)Is it necessary to use weights? Realistically, the use of weights is only necessary if you are using a very lightweight lure, such as 1/16 ounce or any lure that is too small to cast efficiently. You will also want to add more weight in more windy conditions, as the wind will cause the water to move making the lure shallower than desired. Can water and the type of fish effect the weight used? Only you can decide how much weight to use. Can weight actually affect the fish species or weight of the fish caught? Yes, but in specific situations. Larger fish will generally hold in deeper water, so some weight, or more depending on depth, may be required to reach these depths.
